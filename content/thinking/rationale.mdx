# Rationale

The full reasoning lives across the other three layers. Stated plainly, the onboarding is a counterweight to overwhelm: one next step, never many, confidence before connection, invitation rather than notification, named by action and never personified.

> Our best guess, made better by listening.

## Why two layers

The piece worth saying out loud is that the two layers serve different purposes.

The deterministic layer is our best guess of what the leader needs, delivered on a predetermined, trigger-driven cadence. The conversation does something the guess cannot. It surfaces what leaders actually need and where the gaps are, across many conversations, and it gets us closer to the real need before we offer anything.

Together: the deterministic layer moves, and the conversation tells us whether the guess was right and quietly corrects it.

## The anti-dependency stance

This is a deliberate ethical choice. The conversational tool must make the leader more self-sufficient and more surrendered, not more reliant on software. So it has a known, bounded scope made legible through named skills. At the edge of that scope its instinct is to point outward, to a pastor or coordinator and to durable resources, rather than inward to itself. It does not do the formation work for the leader: no generated session plans, no materials that replace engaging the course. And it is never personified or branded as "an AI." This is both an ethical position and a signal of product judgment.

## What the training videos taught me

I watched several of the group leader training videos, honestly not all of them. A few things stood out. The training arcs deliberately, from an overview of the framework, toward *you do not need all the answers*, toward handling difficulty in a group. That arc directly shaped the week one to week three structure.

My own first reaction was mild overwhelm, partly from feeling I had to get through everything at once. That is the origin of the whole thesis. I eased once I had the lay of the land, which validates designing for orientation first, then steps. My instinct was to look for the Companion Guide, so we surface it clearly and early. And the example schedule felt like a lot at once, so we reveal structure progressively rather than dumping it.

## What we chose not to include

Some omissions are decisions of intent: no recruitment path, since we assume an existing group. No countdown, which only manufactures pressure. No capturing of the opening reflective question. No AI persona. No notification badges. No tool that builds the leader's session plans for them.

Others are honest constraints of the exercise: a vector database for grounding the conversation, and real backend signal plumbing, are things we would build in production but not here. The prototype leans on a strong system prompt, a few skills, and static data.

## How we would know it works

Self-reported confidence after the first session, defined in the same terms as the design intent. Healthy signal movement without pressure as the cause. Conversations that leave the leader with one concrete next step or a delivered resource. And a rising share of conversations that end by pointing the leader to real people, evidence that the tool helps without creating dependency.
