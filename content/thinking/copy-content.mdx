# Copy + Content

The team has writers, so copy is not the deliverable here. The direction is. And one sentence governs all of it.

> Communication should promote calm, quiet confidence, even when not everything is known and not everything is done.

The guiding rule for *what* we say is always the next right step.

## The voice, as a filter

Run every line through this. Calm, warm, invitational, not directive. Plain sentences with quiet depth, first-person plural, sentence case. Normalize not-knowing, the signature move for this audience: you do not need to have it all figured out to begin well. Leave room to celebrate, because calm is the baseline, not a ceiling on warmth. No hype, no urgency, no guilt, no jargon. No em dashes. One ask per message. Name the action, never the AI.

## Two hierarchies

**Which one thing, across messages.** We say the single thing that most serves the leader now, in this channel.

- **Primary** is the one next right step. Always exactly one. Confidence before invitation.
- **Secondary** is the conversation, a quiet link to go deeper, never a competing ask.
- **Tertiary** is a reflection or gentle hint, optional and easily ignored.

**Within a single message:** what to do, then what to know, then how to feel, where how to feel is almost always carried by tone rather than stated. We do not write "feel calm." The restraint makes them calm.

## The skeleton

One warm line of acknowledgement, often celebratory. The one thing. A beat that lowers the weight or lifts them up, because it is small and finishable, or because they are more ready than they think. A single action. Then, quietly, the secondary. Reusing this shape across the email and the conversation is what makes the whole experience feel like one voice.

## How the conversation opens

The email's secondary link carries a message pre-filled as the leader, *"I would like to talk through getting ready for my first session."* So the conversation opens with the leader's own words already said, and responds to them. It never opens by greeting or introducing itself.

## A canonical example

The first email, week one, where the primary ask is confidence and not invitation:

> Hi Sarah,
>
> You have said yes to leading your group through the Practicing the Way Course. That is a meaningful, generous thing, and you do not need to have it all figured out to begin well.
>
> There is just one thing worth doing first. Not inviting anyone yet, not planning sessions. Just watching session one of the leader training, about six minutes, helps the shape of what is ahead feel familiar.
>
> **Begin session one.** Not sure where your head is at? *Talk it through.*
>
> Peace to you as you start, the Practicing the Way team.
